For Waverly, it was always Riley.

The truth was that there was little Waverly wanted to think about before Riley Brandon entered her life.

It’s the before that Waverly wished she could forget.

When Eleanor Westley got pregnant during her internship working for Nathanial Vanderbilt’s early political career, she knew she had to keep a secret. In fact, she was paid significantly well to do so. A promoted, paid position on the would-be Senator’s team and a cushy raise was more than enough to buy her silence, all while being able to provide for her daughter. By the time Waverly was born in the morning hours of November 4th, 1996, Eleanor was fairly confident in her ability to care for her daughter as a single parent, all while continuing her aspiring career in the political world.

Or at least, that was the plan until her death just over two years later.

The letter she left before taking her own life explained everything, and Eleanor made sure everyone saw it by mailing copies to any journalist she had made a connection with. Nathanial Vanderbilt’s secret affair with his intern turned campaign manager. The money he used to bribe her with silence. The two year old daughter he didn’t want to acknowledge. It all came out in headlines weeks before an election that would make his career.

An election he lost, and one he would never let that motherless two year old forget.

A scandal could be contained. A blood stain cleaned until the public barely remembered. But Nathanial always remembered. Even if he acknowledged his daughter and gave her his last name, it didn’t change the resentment and anger. He could provide everything for a daughter he had never wanted, from the best nannies to the best schools that money could buy, but that would never mean he loved her.

Only that he had to tolerate her, at least in public, lest the public remember his sins.

Her mother loved her enough to guarantee that.

But that’s the part that Waverly tried not to think about.

Instead, she preferred to think about the blonde girl she met that first day of kindergarten. The one who held out the dandelion yellow crayon with the brightest smile she’d ever seen. The one who had patted the seat next to her at carpet time. The one who told her by lunch that they were the very best of friends.

And meant it.

Riley Brandon was more than just her best friend. Her family became her own. As they grew past elementary school and into their teenage years, there was never an event at the Brandon house that didn’t have a place for Waverly, too. When her father would leave her behind with a nanny to spend holidays in Washington DC, there was a place for Waverly in Riley’s home. Whether that be a second homemade Valentine’s Day box by her mother or a stocking on the mantle at Christmas time, the Brandon’s became the place that Waverly found family for the first time.

It was no surprise they fell in love.

From the time she kissed Riley for the first time - a quiet moment just after the Christmas formal, when Riley had been so tender with taking Waverly’s makeup off that she couldn’t help herself - Waverly knew Nathanial would never approve. He would never stand for another potential blemish on his name, and a lesbian daughter could only be that. But Riley, her patient, lovely, amazing Riley, understood. So they kept it quiet, letting it exist in their private moments together so long as Waverly had to live with Nathanial.

By the time their graduation day came, Waverly didn’t care. She threw her arms around Riley and kissed her in front of everyone, and she didn’t care who saw or who wrote about it.

She didn’t have to live by his rules anymore.

It was the day she chose to live by her own rules and not by the set determined by her father’s reputation.

So Waverly moved in with the Brandon family the day she graduated. She attended school in New York with Riley, forgoing her father’s desires for her to attend Yale as he had and choosing NYU and the art degrees she dreamed of instead. Waverly chose to make a life with Riley, whether that be in Boston or New York City or now, as Riley’s quickly growing modeling career made a move to Boston more practical. She chose Riley, because choosing Riley meant choosing her own happiness above everything else.

Because after all, it had always been Riley.
